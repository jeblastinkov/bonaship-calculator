## Segment A: HOME BUYERS

---

### Archetype A1 — "The Independent First-Timer"
**Priority: PRIMARY (ECP — Early Customer Profile)**

**Who they are:**
Martin, 28. Software developer in Bratislava, renting a 1-room flat with his girlfriend. Net income ~€1,800/month. Has been saving for 2+ years and has ~€12,000 saved. He looked into getting a mortgage, found a property at €140,000, needs €28,000 down (20%) — he's €16,000 short. His parents offered to help but he doesn't want to ask. He hasn't followed up since. Still renting.

**A day in his life:**
Martin opens his banking app, looks at his savings balance, and sighs. He's been doing the math for months. The flat he wants in Ružinov would cost €1,100/month in rent or he could own it — but the deposit is the wall. He's ruled out asking his parents ("that never ends well"), he checked with a mortgage broker who said he doesn't qualify without more down payment, and he's not interested in a consumer loan on top of a mortgage. He feels stuck, frustrated, and slightly embarrassed that at 28, owning a home feels this out of reach.

**Functional goals:** 🔧
- Buy a 2–3 room apartment in Bratislava within the next 12 months
- Qualify for a mortgage as an individual (no co-borrower)
- Close the €10–15K deposit gap without taking a second loan

**Social goals:** 👤
- Be seen as a responsible adult who didn't need his parents' help
- Achieve something his peers haven't yet ("I bought my place without family money")
- Prove that he can handle adult financial decisions independently

**Emotional goals:** ❤️
- Stop feeling stuck or dependent
- Feel secure — own something, stop "throwing money away on rent"
- Reduce housing anxiety; gain a sense of control and forward momentum

**Pains:**
- Undesired outcome: Gets rejected by bank or doesn't even try because the math doesn't work
- Undesired outcome: Has to compromise on apartment quality (smaller, worse location) to meet deposit
- Obstacle: NBS 20% rule means his €12K is not enough — there is no simple workaround
- Obstacle: Adding a consumer loan on top of mortgage increases DTI and makes mortgage harder
- Risk: Involving family creates emotional debt, potential conflict, loss of autonomy
- Risk: "Co-investment" sounds like another trap — possible hidden costs, legal complications

**Gains sought:**
- Required: No monthly payment on the co-investment (can't afford two repayments)
- Required: No credit registry entry (doesn't want a second "debt" impacting future borrowing)
- Expected: Full ownership of the apartment (no shared title with anyone)
- Expected: Clear, transparent exit conditions (knows exactly how and when Bonaship gets paid back)
- Desired: Buy a better apartment than he could afford alone
- Desired: Keep some cash after purchase (emergency fund)
- Unexpected (if offered): Mortgage broker partnership — Bonaship helps him through the whole bank process, not just provides capital

**Current alternatives:**
- Wait and save more (slow, rent continues, prices rise)
- Ask family for a gift or loan (emotionally costly)
- Buy smaller/cheaper (compromises on quality and location)
- Consumer loan as top-up (adds monthly payments, worsens DTI, credit registry)

**Adoption threshold:**
Will say YES if: no monthly payments, not recorded as debt, legal structure is clear, trustworthy (not a non-bank lender), process is digital and fast.
Will say NO if: perceives hidden costs, is asked to co-title the property with a stranger, or the legal complexity feels overwhelming.

**Signal phrases:**
- "I just can't get the 20% together fast enough"
- "I don't want to ask my parents — that never ends well"
- "I know the apartment I want, I just don't have the gap covered"
- "I need something that doesn't add another monthly payment on top of the mortgage"

**Buying power:** Decides independently (no second decision-maker unless in a couple). Mortgage broker may be the gatekeeper.

### Archetype A2 — "The Upgrading Couple"
**Priority: SECONDARY (broader ICP)**

**Who they are:**
Zuzana & Peter, 33 and 35. Both working professionals in Košice, combined income ~€3,800/month. They have €22,000 saved and want to buy a family apartment — their target is €180,000 (3-room, good neighbourhood). They technically qualify for a mortgage but only if they use ALL their savings for the deposit, leaving zero buffer. They're also expecting their first child.

**A day in their life:**
They've been spreadsheet-planning for months. With €22K saved, they can almost make the 20% on a €110K apartment — but that's not the apartment they want. The one they actually want costs €180K, meaning they need €36K minimum. Their parents could contribute, but Zuzana doesn't want to mix family money with their home ("too many opinions"). The clock is ticking — a child on the way, rent going up, and the apartment they want just went back on the market.

**Functional goals:** 🔧
- Buy a specific apartment in a specific neighbourhood before a specific life event (baby)
- Use savings efficiently without depleting emergency fund
- Get into a higher property value bracket than their savings allow

**Social goals:** 👤
- "We did this ourselves" — as a couple, without family involvement
- Buy a place their friends would genuinely respect (location, size, quality)
- Feel like responsible future parents making the right financial move

**Emotional goals:** ❤️
- Reduce timing anxiety (baby coming, need to be settled)
- Feel financially secure post-purchase (not emptying savings)
- Gain a sense of "we made it" — homeowners before becoming parents

**Pains:**
- Undesired outcome: Depletes entire savings for deposit → zero buffer with baby on the way
- Undesired outcome: Settles for a smaller/worse apartment they'll outgrow quickly
- Obstacle: Combined income is strong but savings are just short of what the better apartment requires
- Risk: Post-purchase financial vulnerability with a new child and zero cash reserve

**Gains sought:**
- Required: No monthly payment on Bonaship's portion (budget already maxed with mortgage)
- Required: Can maintain at least €8–10K in savings after closing
- Expected: Full flexibility on apartment choice (no Bonaship veto on which property to buy)
- Desired: Access to a property at least €30–40K above what they could afford unaided
- Desired: Simple, fast process (they're busy, one child already in the picture)

**Current alternatives:**
- Accept family contribution (emotionally fraught)
- Buy smaller and plan to upgrade in 5 years (inefficient, but common fallback)
- Wait another 2–3 years to save more (not viable with baby timeline)

**Adoption threshold:**
Will say YES if: can buy the apartment they actually want, keep cash buffer, no ongoing payment. Will say NO if: involves complex legal structures they need to explain to a notary or their parents.

**Signal phrases:**
- "We'd buy in a heartbeat if we could just cover the gap"
- "We don't want to drain all our savings and have nothing left"
- "We want the apartment we actually want, not the one we can barely afford"

**Buying power:** Joint decision. Both need to be aligned. Mortgage broker likely involved.

## Segment B: HOME OWNERS

---

### Archetype B1 — "The Debt-Trapped Homeowner"
**Priority: PRIMARY (ECP — Early Customer Profile)**

**Who they are:**
Jana, 47. Elementary school teacher in Bratislava. Owns a 3-room apartment she bought 12 years ago (now worth ~€160,000, 55% equity). She has consumer loan debt totalling ~€22,000 at 18% p.a., which costs her ~€550/month. A divorce two years ago left her restructuring finances solo. She tried refinancing through her bank — they rejected her due to DTI limits. She feels trapped.

**A day in her life:**
Jana checks her salary credit notification. €1,650 net. €550 goes out immediately on loan repayments. €600 for the flat maintenance and utilities. What's left doesn't cover everything she needs for her daughter's school and their lifestyle. She's considered selling the apartment and renting — but that feels like going backward after 12 years. She's heard of "non-bank lenders" but knows they're predatory. She doesn't know what else to do. Her equity is sitting there, locked, while her credit score slowly deteriorates.

**Functional goals:** 🔧
- Consolidate €22K consumer debt into a single solution with no new monthly repayment
- Free up monthly cash flow (~€550/month currently going to loans)
- Do this without selling the apartment or taking on a new loan

**Social goals:** 👤
- Not appear financially distressed to family and colleagues
- Solve this quietly, without involving family or admitting the depth of the problem
- Maintain the image of someone managing their finances responsibly

**Emotional goals:** ❤️
- Relieve the constant financial stress and anxiety
- Feel in control again — not reactive to every unexpected expense
- Avoid the shame of perceived financial failure (divorce, debt, bank rejection)

**Pains:**
- Undesired outcome: Bank rejected her — no conventional path exists
- Undesired outcome: Non-bank lenders are a trap that would worsen her position
- Obstacle: NBS DTI limits prevent adding more debt — she's maxed out formally
- Obstacle: Doesn't have liquid savings to bridge any gap herself
- Risk: If she takes a non-bank loan, she falls deeper into the spiral
- Risk: If she sells the apartment to clear debt, she loses her primary asset and goes back to renting
- Risk: "What if I can't buy Bonaship back?" — fear of losing her home long-term

**Gains sought:**
- Required: No new monthly payment (the whole point is to reduce cash outflow)
- Required: Not recorded in credit databases (she needs to protect her credit for future)
- Required: Clear exit terms — she needs to know exactly how and when this ends
- Expected: Fast access to funds — the debt stress is urgent
- Desired: A trusted, professional process — not something that feels predatory
- Desired: The ability to keep her apartment and pass it to her daughter
- Unexpected: Educational content that explains her full options — she feels uninformed

**Current alternatives:**
- Non-bank lenders (knows they're bad, won't use)
- Selling the apartment (emotionally devastating, last resort)
- Living with debt (current state — unsustainable)
- Asking family (too much shame involved)

**Adoption threshold:**
Will say YES if: no monthly payment, no credit registry, fast process, trustworthy brand (feels institutional, not predatory), clear legal protection. Will say NO if: feels like a trap, hidden fees, or she doesn't fully understand the exit mechanics.

**Signal phrases:**
- "The bank said no — I've tried"
- "I can't take another monthly payment"
- "I have equity in this apartment but I can't get to it"
- "I don't want to go to a non-bank lender — that's worse"
- "I just want to get out from under this debt"

**Buying power:** Single decision-maker. High urgency. Will move fast if trust is established.

### Archetype B2 — "The Equity Optimizer"
**Priority: SECONDARY (broader ICP)**

**Who they are:**
Tomáš, 53. Senior manager at a logistics company, Bratislava. Owns a 4-room apartment worth ~€280,000 (bought for €120K in 2007, 75% equity). No mortgage remaining. Has a comfortable income (€3,200/month net) but limited liquid savings. He wants to buy a second property as a rental investment but doesn't have the 20% deposit (~€40–50K). He doesn't want a new mortgage — he's debt-free and wants to stay that way. He's been exploring options for 6 months.

**A day in his life:**
Tomáš reads property market reports over breakfast. He knows the Bratislava rental market is strong. He's done the math: a €200K 2-room apartment in Petržalka would yield €800/month in rent — a decent return. His flat has €200K+ of equity sitting idle. His accountant mentioned HEI products exist in Germany. He's intrigued. His bank offered him a home equity loan but the monthly repayment would eat most of the rental yield and it goes on his credit record. He wants something cleaner.

**Functional goals:** 🔧
- Unlock ~€40–50K from his property equity to fund a second property deposit
- Avoid creating a monthly repayment obligation
- Maintain his debt-free status (no credit registry entry)

**Social goals:** 👤
- Be seen as a sophisticated investor who knows how to leverage assets intelligently
- Make a financially smart move — not the obvious bank loan route
- Peer validation: "I did something clever with my equity"

**Emotional goals:** ❤️
- Feel like he's capitalizing on his life's financial progress (20+ years building equity)
- Gain confidence that his wealth is working for him, not sitting idle
- Avoid the anxiety of new debt commitment on top of a speculative investment

**Pains:**
- Undesired outcome: Taking a bank equity loan creates monthly repayment that eats the rental yield
- Obstacle: He doesn't have liquid savings for the second deposit — it's all locked in the apartment
- Obstacle: Standard HELOC/HEL adds to credit record — he doesn't want that
- Risk: Second property investment risk is real — if the rental market drops, he's exposed on two fronts
- Risk: Exit terms with Bonaship might be complex to explain to future buyers of his property

**Gains sought:**
- Required: No monthly repayment (must not erode rental yield)
- Required: Clear exit mechanics — he's analytical, needs to model the scenarios
- Expected: Flexible timing — he wants to hold the second property 10–15 years before selling
- Desired: A digital calculator / modelling tool to see the exact numbers before committing
- Desired: Ability to buy Bonaship out early if he has a windfall or refinances the second property

**Current alternatives:**
- Bank home equity loan (available but creates monthly payment and credit entry)
- Sell his apartment and buy outright (would lose his primary home)
- Wait and continue saving (15+ years to get €50K from salary alone)
- Sell a portion of his apartment (sale & leaseback — exists but extreme, would mean losing title)

**Adoption threshold:**
Will say YES if: can model the numbers clearly, exit mechanics are transparent, brand is institutional/professional. Will say NO if: he can't find a clear comparable elsewhere in Europe, or the legal complexity feels uncertain.

**Signal phrases:**
- "I have the equity, I just can't access it cleanly"
- "A bank loan would eat the rental yield"
- "I want something that doesn't show up on my credit record"
- "I've seen this model in Germany — is there something like that here?"

**Buying power:** Single decision-maker. Deliberate, analytical, will do due diligence. Longer sales cycle than B1. Worth pursuing for larger deal size.
